# Product

## Register

product

## Users

Casual car owners who want a clear picture of what their car costs them. Not fleet managers or data obsessives. Someone who fills up, gets an oil change, and wants those moments captured without friction. They open the app briefly, often right after an event (just fueled up, just left the shop), then close it. The interface should feel like it already knows what they came to do and gets out of the way.

## Product Purpose

AutoMate tracks car expenses (fuel fill-ups, service, and other costs) and turns them into a readable financial and maintenance history. Success: a user can answer "what did this car cost me last year?" and "when is my next oil change?" without digging through receipts.

## Brand Personality

Three archetypes, blended:

1. **The sharp friend** who remembers everything about your car so you don't have to. Anticipates what you need, pre-fills what it can, never makes you repeat yourself.
2. **The no-nonsense accountant** who tells you exactly where your money went. Clear, precise, trustworthy with numbers. No filler, no fluff.
3. **The dapper sophisticate** who always dresses to impress. Every surface is considered. Details are polished. The app looks like it costs more than it does.

Voice: confident, precise, warm but not chatty. Copy is short and declarative. Labels do the talking; paragraphs are suspect.

Three-word personality: **sharp, polished, composed.**

Emotional target: **clarity, confidence, peace of mind.** When a user opens AutoMate after filling up, they should feel "I'm in control of this, and this app respects my time and taste."

## References

- **Monarch Money** -- restrained palette, editorial quality, soft card shadows, gradient trend lines. The airy spaciousness and muted category colors. Proof that a finance app can feel premium without dark mode theatrics.
- **YNAB** -- confident density when appropriate, tabular number alignment, horizontal progress bars per category. The budget grid embraces data without apologizing for it.
- **Hurdlr** -- big hero numbers, one-metric-per-card dashboard, weight contrast (light labels, heavy values). The "live scoreboard" feel of the top-level metrics.
- **Todoist** -- functional minimalism, intelligent quick-add defaults, feels like a personal assistant not a database. Subtle color coding without clutter.

## Anti-references

- **Mint** -- dashboard crammed everything (budgets, accounts, bills, credit score, ads) into one scroll with no hierarchy. Garish category colors. Ads disguised as features ("Ways to Save"). Generated anxiety, not confidence.
- **Quicken Mobile** -- desktop spreadsheet forced onto a phone. Dense tables with tiny text, no whitespace, excessive decimal precision. Gray gradients and drop shadows. Felt like enterprise accounting software.
- **Generic auto tracker aesthetic** -- the category-reflex of navy + gold, grey dashboards, speedometer gauges, or car-shaped icons used as decoration. AutoMate is a financial tool that happens to track cars, not a car app that happens to show money.

## Design Principles

1. **The app does the thinking.** Intelligent defaults, inferred values, pre-filled fields. The user confirms, not constructs.
2. **Clarity over density.** Every screen communicates in seconds. Numbers, not paragraphs. Hierarchy that guides the eye. Three to four focused metrics per view, never a wall of data.
3. **Dress to impress.** Surfaces, spacing, and typography are considered down to the pixel. The app should look like it was designed by someone who cares, for someone who notices.
4. **Progressive disclosure.** Show what's needed for the current task. One question at a time. Complexity exists but stays behind the curtain until summoned.
5. **Context-aware surfaces.** The right tools appear for the right task. Forms adapt. Empty states explain. Errors guide rather than scold.
